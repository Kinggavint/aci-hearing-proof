Balance Testing

Comprehensive vestibular evaluation to identify the root cause of dizziness and balance problems.

Understanding Dizziness & Balance Problems

Dizziness and balance problems are among the most common complaints affecting adults, yet they are often under-diagnosed or mismanaged. The sensation of spinning, lightheadedness, or unsteadiness can significantly interfere with daily activities and raise the risk of dangerous falls.

At ACI Hearing Center, our audiologists are specially trained in vestibular assessment. A thorough evaluation is the critical first step in finding answers and getting the right treatment.

Common Causes of Dizziness

Many conditions can cause dizziness and balance disturbances. Some of the most frequent causes include:

Inner Ear Causes

  • BPPV (Benign Paroxysmal Positional Vertigo): Loose calcium crystals (otoconia) in the inner ear — the most common cause of vertigo
  • Weakness in balance organs: Reduced function in one or both vestibular organs
  • Ménière's disease: Excess fluid pressure in the inner ear causing episodic vertigo
  • Untreated hearing loss: Can contribute to balance difficulty and spatial disorientation

Other Contributing Factors

  • Migraines: Vestibular migraines can cause episodes of dizziness and vertigo
  • Blood pressure fluctuations: Orthostatic hypotension and cardiovascular factors
  • Diabetes & neuropathy: Peripheral neuropathy can impair balance and proprioception
  • Medications: Many common medications list dizziness as a side effect

Vestibular Testing at ACI

Our balance evaluations are comprehensive and designed to identify the source of your symptoms. We use advanced diagnostic equipment to objectively assess the vestibular system:

Videonystagmography (VNG)

VNG is the gold-standard diagnostic test for evaluating inner ear function and the neurological pathways involved in balance. Using lightweight video goggles, we record and analyze eye movements in response to specific head positions and temperature changes in the ear canal.

VNG testing helps us determine:

  • Whether dizziness originates in the inner ear or the central nervous system
  • Which ear is affected and to what degree
  • The specific type of vestibular disorder present
  • The most appropriate course of treatment

BPPV Treatment: The Epley Maneuver

BPPV (Benign Paroxysmal Positional Vertigo) is the single most common cause of vertigo. It occurs when small calcium crystals (called otoconia or "ear rocks") become dislodged from their normal position and migrate into the semicircular canals of the inner ear.

The good news: BPPV is highly treatable without medication or surgery. The Epley maneuver and other repositioning techniques guide the displaced crystals back to their proper location. Most patients experience significant relief after just one or two sessions.

Our audiologists, including Drs. Sarah Lalande and Elizabeth Lemaire, are experienced in diagnosing and treating BPPV and other vestibular disorders in both adults and children.

What to Expect During Your Evaluation

  1. Patient history — We begin with a detailed discussion of your symptoms, their timing, triggers, and any factors that worsen or relieve them.
  2. Positional testing — We observe how your eyes and body respond to specific head and body position changes.
  3. VNG testing — Objective measurement of vestibular function using video goggles and targeted stimuli.
  4. Results review — We explain your test results in plain language and discuss what they mean for your condition.
  5. Treatment & referral — Depending on your diagnosis, we may provide in-office treatment (such as the Epley maneuver), recommend vestibular rehabilitation, or coordinate with your physician or ENT for further care.

When Should You Seek Evaluation?

Schedule a balance evaluation if you experience any of the following:

  • Sudden episodes of spinning or vertigo, especially with head movements
  • Chronic dizziness, lightheadedness, or feeling off-balance
  • Difficulty walking in the dark or on uneven surfaces
  • Falls or near-falls without a clear cause
  • Dizziness accompanied by hearing loss, ear fullness, or ringing
  • Nausea and vomiting triggered by head movement
